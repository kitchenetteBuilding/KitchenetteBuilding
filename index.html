During the 1960s, life was not easy for African Americans. They were treated with little respect and each day was a fight for their independence and freedom. There were many influential people who played a motivational role for African Americans. While some people used violence to gain their rights, other people used their voice and words to have an impact on the world. Poets were inspiring and many people wrote poems as a way to express what their voices could not. One poet in particular, Gwendolyn Brooks, used her strong words to exemplify what living conditions were like for African Americans during the 1960s. Within her poem kitchenette building, she illustrates how the thought of a dream could become a reality. Brooks uses imagery involving cooking and colors to explain her thoughts. Throughout the poem, the author portrays how much responsibility a dream takes, but it seems almost impossible due to the harsh living conditions that the people live in. The poem comes to a halt at lines instead of thirteen, which is the length of a regular sonnet. This depicts that there is room at the end of the poem for hope, that a dream will become a reality. Despite the harsh reality the author faces, she has a positive outlook that a dream is possible.
